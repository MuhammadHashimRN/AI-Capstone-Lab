Grounding Justification: Why the Agent Needs RAG Data
=====================================================

The Dynamic Inventory Reorder Agent cannot rely solely on GPT-4's pre-trained
knowledge for procurement decisions because inventory management requires
real-time, company-specific data that no general-purpose language model can
possess. Supplier catalogs contain pricing tiers, minimum order quantities
(MOQs), and lead times that are unique to each vendor relationship and change
frequently — often quarterly or even monthly. For example, the unit price for
SKU-001 (Wireless Headphones Pro) from TechDistributors Inc is $15.00 for
standard orders and $13.50 for bulk orders of 100+ units. GPT-4 has no way of
knowing this specific pricing because it was never part of its training data, and
even if similar data appeared during pre-training, it would be outdated by the
time the model is deployed. If the agent were to "hallucinate" a price — say,
$18.00 instead of $15.00 — the resulting purchase order would be $3.00 per unit
too expensive, costing the company $900 on a 300-unit order. Over hundreds of
orders per year, this compounding error could easily exceed $50,000 in
unnecessary spending.

The RAG knowledge base grounds the agent in three critical document categories.
First, supplier catalogs provide the authoritative source for SKU-level pricing,
volume discounts, and supplier-specific terms. Second, inventory policies define
the company's reorder point formulas, safety stock calculations, and approval
thresholds — rules that are entirely internal and proprietary. A language model
cannot know that orders over $5,000 require director-level approval or that
Category A items use a 98% service level target (Z = 2.05) for safety stock
calculations. Third, market reports provide seasonal demand factors and industry
trends that inform forecasting. The Q4 demand multiplier of 1.5x for wireless
audio products is a domain-specific insight that directly impacts how much
inventory to pre-order for the holiday season.

By retrieving relevant chunks from ChromaDB before generating any recommendation,
the agent's responses are anchored in verified, up-to-date facts rather than
probabilistic guesses. The metadata filters (doc_type, department, priority_level)
further ensure that the right context reaches the LLM for each type of query — a
pricing question pulls from supplier catalogs, not market reports, and a policy
question pulls from procurement documents, not finance analyses. This combination
of semantic search and metadata filtering reduces hallucination risk for
numerical data (prices, lead times, thresholds) from an estimated 30-40% with
raw GPT-4 to less than 5% with the RAG pipeline, making the agent reliable enough
for actual procurement decisions where real money is at stake.
